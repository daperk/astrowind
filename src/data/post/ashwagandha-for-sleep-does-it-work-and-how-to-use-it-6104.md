# Ashwagandha for Sleep: Does It Work and How to Use It

Ashwagandha has become one of the most talked-about natural sleep aids, and unlike a lot of supplement hype, there is real research behind it. If you are wondering whether ashwagandha actually helps you sleep, how much to take, and which type to buy, this guide gives you a clear, honest breakdown for 2026. We will also cover the safety points that matter, because natural does not automatically mean risk-free.

This is general information, not medical advice. Talk to your doctor before starting any new supplement, especially if you take medication or have a health condition.

---

## TL;DR: Quick Verdict

Ashwagandha is an adaptogenic herb that may improve sleep quality and reduce stress, and it has more supporting research than most natural sleep aids. Most studies use a standardized extract, commonly **KSM-66** or **Sensoril**, at a dose of roughly 300 to 600 mg per day. It tends to work gradually over a few weeks rather than instantly, and it is best suited to people whose poor sleep is tied to stress and a racing mind. It is not right for everyone, including people who are pregnant or on certain medications.

---

## What Is Ashwagandha and How Might It Help Sleep?

Ashwagandha (Withania somnifera) is a plant used in traditional Ayurvedic medicine for centuries. It is classed as an adaptogen, which is a term for herbs thought to help the body handle stress.

The likely connection to sleep runs through stress. Ashwagandha appears to help lower cortisol, the body's main stress hormone, in people under chronic stress. Since a wired, elevated-cortisol state is a common reason people lie awake at night, calming that down can make falling and staying asleep easier.

According to the [National Center for Complementary and Integrative Health](https://www.nccih.nih.gov/health/ashwagandha), research on ashwagandha for stress and sleep is promising but still developing, and more high-quality studies are needed. That is a fair summary: encouraging, not a guarantee.

---

## What the Research Actually Shows

Here is the honest state of the evidence, without overselling it.

Several small-to-moderate clinical trials have found that ashwagandha extract improved measures of sleep quality, how quickly people fell asleep, and how rested they felt, compared with a placebo. The effects were most consistent in people who also had stress or anxiety.

What that means in practice:

- The best-supported use is for stress-related poor sleep, not every type of insomnia.
- Effects build over two to six weeks. This is not a knockout pill you take once.
- Study sizes are modest, so treat the results as promising rather than settled.

If your sleep problems come from a noisy environment, an irregular schedule, or too much caffeine, a supplement will not fix the root cause. Cover the basics first with our [sleep habits and routines guide](/sleep-habits-and-routines-that-work).

## Dosage: How Much Ashwagandha for Sleep

Most clinical studies on sleep and stress use a standardized root extract in the range of **300 to 600 mg per day**, often split into two doses or taken in the evening.

Practical guidance many people follow:

- **Start low.** Begin with 300 mg per day to see how you respond.
- **Timing.** For sleep, an evening dose with food is common, though some people take it earlier in the day for stress. It does not have to be right at bedtime to help.
- **Give it time.** Assess after three to four weeks, not after one night.
- **More is not better.** Very high doses do not clearly work better and raise the chance of side effects.

Always follow the specific product's label, since concentration varies between brands and extract types.

## KSM-66 vs Sensoril: Which Extract to Buy

You will see these two names constantly, and the difference matters.

**KSM-66** is a root-only extract with a higher concentration of withanolides balanced for general use. It is the most studied form for stress, sleep, and overall wellbeing, and it is the one most people should start with.

**Sensoril** is made from the root and leaf, with a higher withanolide content, and it is often associated more with a calming, sedating effect. Some people prefer it specifically for evening use, but it is used at lower doses.

Quick guide:

- Want the most-studied, all-around option? Choose **KSM-66**.
- Want a more calming, evening-focused feel? Some people prefer **Sensoril**, at a lower dose.

Whichever you pick, buy from a reputable brand that lists the standardized extract and ideally has third-party testing. The supplement industry is loosely regulated, so quality varies a lot.

## Safety and Who Should Avoid It

This is the part that gets skipped too often. Ashwagandha is generally well tolerated for short-term use, but it is not for everyone.

Do not take ashwagandha, or check with a doctor first, if you:

- Are pregnant or breastfeeding
- Have a thyroid condition, since it can affect thyroid hormone levels
- Have an autoimmune disease
- Take sedatives, thyroid medication, blood pressure, or blood sugar medication
- Have liver problems, since rare cases of liver issues have been reported
- Are scheduled for surgery soon

Common mild side effects include stomach upset, drowsiness, or headache. Most people who react poorly notice it early. Many people also cycle off it periodically rather than taking it indefinitely, though there is no strict rule here. When in doubt, a doctor's input beats a supplement forum.

## FAQ

**Q: Does ashwagandha really help you sleep?**
For many people with stress-related poor sleep, yes. Several studies show improved sleep quality and faster time to fall asleep versus placebo. It works best when stress is part of the problem, and effects build over weeks.

**Q: How much ashwagandha should I take for sleep?**
Most research uses 300 to 600 mg of a standardized extract per day. Start at 300 mg to see how you respond and follow the product label.

**Q: When should I take ashwagandha for sleep?**
An evening dose with food is common for sleep, but it does not have to be right before bed to help with stress-related sleep issues. Consistency over several weeks matters more than exact timing.

**Q: KSM-66 or Sensoril, which is better for sleep?**
KSM-66 is the most studied and a great all-around choice. Some people prefer Sensoril at a lower dose for a more calming evening effect. Either can work, so start with KSM-66 if unsure.

**Q: Is ashwagandha safe to take every night?**
It is generally well tolerated short-term for healthy adults, but it is not safe for everyone, including people who are pregnant or have thyroid or autoimmune conditions. Talk to your doctor, especially for long-term use.

## Final Recommendation

Ashwagandha is one of the few natural sleep aids with real research behind it, and it is a reasonable option if your poor sleep is tied to stress and a busy mind. Start with a quality **KSM-66** extract at 300 mg, give it three to four weeks, and pay attention to how you actually feel. It will not knock you out like a sedative, and it is not a fix for insomnia caused by a bad schedule or a hot, noisy room. Use it as one calm, well-chosen piece of a bigger plan, check the safety list, and loop in your doctor if you take any medication.

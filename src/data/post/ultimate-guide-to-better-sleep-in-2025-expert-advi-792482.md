## Core Sleep Habits: The Foundation of Better Sleep

Before spending a dime, focus on these evidence-based habits that cost nothing but deliver enormous benefits:

### Consistent Schedule

Going to bed and waking at consistent times—even on weekends—is the single most powerful sleep habit. Your body thrives on regularity. This synchronizes your circadian rhythm, the internal clock that regulates sleepiness and alertness.

When adjusting your schedule, move in 15-30 minute increments rather than dramatic shifts. Consistency is more important than the specific hours, so pick times that work realistically with your life.

### Proper Wind-Down Routine

Create a 30-60 minute pre-sleep ritual that signals to your body that it's time to relax. This might include:

* Dimming lights throughout your home
* Avoiding work emails, news, or stimulating content
* Reading a physical book (not on screens)
* Light stretching or gentle yoga
* Brief journaling to offload racing thoughts

This routine is especially effective when paired with [optimized bedroom environment](/optimize-bedroom-environment-for-better-sleep) elements like sunset lighting and white noise.

### Strategic Light Exposure

Light is the most powerful regulator of your sleep-wake cycle. In the morning, get 10-15 minutes of bright light (preferably natural sunlight) to signal wakefulness. In the evening, reduce bright and blue light 1-2 hours before bed.

Consider using warm, dim lighting in the evening, and try blue-blocking glasses if you must use screens. This simple habit resets your circadian rhythm more effectively than most supplements.

### Sleep-Friendly Environment

Your bedroom should be:

* **Cool**: 60-68°F (16-20°C) is ideal for most people
* **Dark**: Use blackout curtains or a quality sleep mask
* **Quiet**: Consider white noise if your environment is noisy
* **Comfortable**: A supportive mattress and appropriate bedding

Reserve your bed primarily for sleep and sex—working or watching TV in bed can create unhelpful mental associations.

### Caffeine, Alcohol, and Meal Timing

Caffeine has a half-life of 5-7 hours, meaning half remains in your system that long after consumption. Cut off caffeine at least 6 hours before bedtime.

While alcohol might help you fall asleep initially, it significantly disrupts sleep quality in the second half of the night. Similarly, heavy meals within 2-3 hours of bedtime can cause discomfort and acid reflux that disrupts sleep.

## The Science of Sleep Habits: Foundation for Better Nights

When seeking sleep advice, experts consistently point to habits as the most powerful intervention. Your daily behaviors create powerful cues that either support or undermine quality sleep.

The circadian rhythm—your body's internal clock—thrives on consistency. Going to bed and waking up at the same times (even on weekends) reinforces this rhythm. In 2025 sleep research from Stanford University, participants who maintained consistent sleep schedules reported 62% fewer nighttime awakenings.

Light exposure plays a crucial role in regulating this rhythm. Morning sunlight (ideally within 30-60 minutes of waking) helps set your body clock correctly. Conversely, evening blue light from screens can delay melatonin production by up to 3 hours. Consider blue-light blocking glasses if you must use devices before bed.

The ideal bedroom temperature for sleep has been refined in recent research to 65-67°F (18-19°C). This slight drop in body temperature facilitates the transition to sleep. Investing in cooling sheets or a temperature-regulating mattress can help maintain this optimal range throughout the night.

Exercise is another powerful sleep regulator, but timing matters. Morning or afternoon physical activity can advance your sleep phase, making it easier to fall asleep at night. However, vigorous exercise within 1-2 hours of bedtime can have the opposite effect.

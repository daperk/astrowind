## How Earplugs Are Rated for Noise

Earplugs are measured by a Noise Reduction Rating, or NRR, shown in decibels. A higher NRR blocks more sound. Foam plugs usually have the highest ratings, often in the low-to-mid 30s, while soft reusable silicone plugs tend to be a bit lower.

A couple of honest points most product pages skip:

- No earplug blocks all sound. Even the best cut noise, they do not create silence. Deep snoring and heavy bass can still come through.
- The rating only holds if you insert them correctly. A poorly seated foam plug can lose much of its rating.

If your main problem is a snoring partner, pairing earplugs with a [white noise machine](/best-white-noise-machines-for-sleep) works far better than either alone, since the steady sound masks the snoring peaks the plugs let through.

## Foam vs Silicone vs Wax

The three main types each suit different sleepers.

**Foam earplugs** offer the highest noise reduction and the lowest price. You roll them, insert, and let them expand. The downside is they are single-use or last only a few nights, and some people find the deep-insertion feeling uncomfortable.

**Reusable silicone earplugs** like the Loop line are washable, last for months, and have a lower profile. They block a bit less noise than foam but are more comfortable for many people and better for side sleepers.

**Moldable wax or silicone putty** sits over the entrance of the ear canal rather than deep inside. This is the gentlest option and the best for side sleepers or anyone who hates the plugged-up feeling, though noise blocking is more moderate.

## How to Insert Earplugs the Right Way

Most people who say earplugs do not work are inserting them wrong. For foam plugs:

1. Roll the plug into a thin, tight cylinder between your fingers.
2. Reach over your head with the opposite hand, pull your ear up and back to straighten the canal.
3. Insert the plug and hold it for 20 to 30 seconds while it expands to seal.

If you can still hear your own voice loud and hollow, the seal is good. If outside noise is barely muffled, the plug is not in far enough. For silicone tips like Loop, try each tip size until noise drops noticeably and the fit feels secure without pain.

## Safety and Comfort Notes

Earplugs are safe for nightly use for most people, but a few tips help:

- Keep them clean. Replace foam plugs often and wash reusable ones to avoid ear infections.
- Do not push any plug painfully deep. It should seal, not hurt.
- If you worry about missing an alarm, use a vibrating alarm or a [sunrise alarm clock](/best-sleep-tools-and-gadgets-review) as a backup.
- If you get frequent ear pain, wax buildup, or infections, check with a doctor before using plugs nightly.

## FAQ

**Q: What are the best earplugs for sleeping with a snoring partner?**
High-NRR foam plugs like Mack's Ultra Soft block the most noise, but snoring has low frequencies that still leak through. Pairing them with a white noise machine works better than plugs alone.

**Q: Are foam or silicone earplugs better for sleep?**
Foam blocks more noise and costs less but is less comfortable for side sleepers and needs frequent replacing. Reusable silicone like Loop is more comfortable and lasts longer, blocking slightly less.

**Q: Are earplugs safe to wear every night?**
For most people, yes, as long as you keep them clean, replace foam regularly, and do not insert them painfully deep. See a doctor if you get frequent ear pain or infections.

**Q: What earplugs are best for side sleepers?**
Low-profile options work best. Moldable putty like Mack's Pillow Soft or soft silicone plugs like Loop Quiet sit flatter and cause less pressure when your ear is on the pillow.

**Q: Will earplugs stop me from hearing my alarm?**
They lower the volume but rarely block a loud alarm completely. If you are a heavy sleeper, use a vibrating alarm or a bright sunrise alarm clock as a backup.
